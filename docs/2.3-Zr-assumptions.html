Continuous predictors

Correlate this

Published

July 18, 2026

Zr is the dominant effect size for describing correlations between two continuous variables. Zr takes the correlation coefficient and sample size as its inputs. The point estimate itself is the arc-tangent of the correlation coefficient (which must be bounded between -1 and 1). See Appendix ((sec?)) for sampling variance formulas.

\[\begin{aligned} &r_{i} \leftarrow \min(1, \max(-1, r_i))\\ &Z_{r_i} = atanh(r_{i})\\ \end{aligned}\] The sampling variance of Zr depends entirely on sample size and is defined as:

\[Var(Z_r) = \frac{1}{n-3}\] As you can see, sampling variance is only calculable if \(n>3\).

In escalc, this looks like:

escalc(ri = 0.7, ni = 15,
       measure = "ZCOR")

      yi     vi 
1 0.8673 0.0833

Zr has among the least biased of point estimate and variance formulas.

Other correlation types

Point-biserial correlations & biserial correlations

Point-biserial and biserial correlations are oddballs. They are very similar to SMD, in that they are an effect size that summarizes the relationship between a discrete predictor and a continuous outcome variable. However, these effect sizes are a measure of association/correlation and range from -1 to 1, instead of describing the difference in magnitude and direction. Point-biserial correlations are for truly distinct predictor variables while biserial correlations are for continuous variables that have been turned into discrete variables (e.g., large versus small).

In our mind, the biggest advantage of these correlations is that they require fewer assumptions to convert to Zr. For example, if you are doing a meta-analysis where the majority of your data are correlations except for one or two studies that use discrete predictors, it may be better to calculate point-biserial or serial correlations for these studies. escalc provides a shorthand to directly convert these statistics to Zr:

# For the point-biserial correlation coefficient (truly discrete groups), directly to `Zr`
escalc(measure = "ZPB",
      m1i = 13.5, m2i = 10.1,
      sd1i = 1.5, sd2i = 2.1,
      n1i = 15, n2i = 15)

      yi     vi 
1 0.8559 0.0265 
# For the biserial correlation coefficient (for a continuous predictor that was discretized), directly to `Zr`
escalc(measure = "ZBIS",
      m1i = 13.5, m2i = 10.1,
      sd1i = 1.5, sd2i = 2.1,
      n1i = 15, n2i = 15)

      yi     vi 
1 1.3330 0.1467
