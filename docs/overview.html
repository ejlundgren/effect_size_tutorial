A brief introduction to effect sizes and sampling variance

Effect sizes are statistics calculated from the results of empirical research that report the variables of interest and, if the effect size is standardized, allow comparison across different studies even if the articles collected data with different units. The effect sizes we’ll discuss in this tutorial concern relationships between 2 variables, however there are other effect sizes for describing univariate results, such as proportions, which we briefly cover in the appendix ((sec?)).

Effect sizes are also known as ‘point estimates’—e.g., the central tendency of a relationship of interest.

Every meta-analytic effect size is associated with sampling variance. Sampling variance describes the estimated variability around the effect size point estimate and is used as a weight in meta-analytic models. Sampling variance is is generally calculated from the sample size and standard deviation of the source data.

Effect size types

We’ll here commit the majority of this tutorial to effect sizes that are concerned with the difference in central tendencies (e.g., means) of a response variable in relation to a categorical or continuous predictor variable. There are other effect sizes for other types of questions, such as estimating differences in heterogeneity between two groups (lnCVR), effect magnitude regardless of direction (lnM), and heterozygosity in population genetics (HWE). These, alongside other, less common effect sizes for meta-analyzing single-group descriptive statistics are briefly presented in a final section ((sec?)). There are also methods for creating custom effect sizes which are covered in a separate tutorial (github.com/ejlundgren/SAFE/XXXX.html).

We here focus this tutorial on the mathematical assumptions, alternative formulations of, and extraction challenges of the 3 most popular effect size types in ecology and evolution:

  1. standardized mean difference (SMD), log-transformed ratio of means (lnRoM), and Zmu ((sec?))

  2. log odds ratio (lnOR) and log risk ratio (lnRR) ((sec?))

  3. correlation coefficient (Zr) ((sec?))

These three effect size classes are determined by the types of data involved in your research question. In particular, whether the response and predictors variables are continuous or discrete.

When the predictor is discrete, for instance between a control and experimental treatment with a continuous response variable, then the effect size is SMD or lnRoM or Zmu-which differ in their interpretability and mathematical assumptions.

If the predictor is discrete (control versus treatment) and the response is a binary outcome, e.g., the number or percent of nests that fledged, then we analyze the data as lnOR or lnRR. This type of effect size can also be used for presence/absence data between two conditions. For example, the presence or absence of a rare bird species on islands with and without introduced predators, where the number of islands in each category (with vs without predator and with vs without bird species) become the discrete outcome (see (sec?)).

The correlation between continuous predictors and continuous responses are analyzed as Zr effect sizes. Note that there are also correlation effect sizes (point-biserial and biserial correlations) for discrete predictors that are readily convertible to Zr and may be preferable to SMD/lnRoM/Zmu effect sizes in some cases ((sec?)).

Finally, it is also possible to convert between these main effect size types, which we discuss at the end ((sec?)). This may be important when you find yourself with limited data: if your dataset is small, sticking to one effect size type may bias your results by excluding alternative experimental designs / data types (ref to borenstein). However, we suggest that care should be taken in ensuring that data is comparable, especially in terms of sample size. We’ll discuss this in the final section ((sec?)).

Calculating effect sizes & sampling variance

The dominant function used to calculate effect sizes and sampling variance in R is the function escalc in the package metafor (REF). escalc takes summary statistics, a variable specifying the effect size type (specified with the measure argument) and returns the effect size point estimate and sampling variance (which we’ll define in further detail below). At certain points we will also use our own custom function, eff_this (currently called eff_size but eff_this could be funny…) which can do most of what escalc can do but also some things that escalc cannot do.

Estimates and estimators

Effect sizes (also called point estimates) and sampling variance are estimated with a variety of estimators. These estimators are the formulas (or methods) used to calculate point estimates and sampling variance for each effect size. Because some estimators can be biased at low sample size, or with heteroscedastic data (e.g., different variance between treatment groups), alternative estimators have been developed for the same effect sizes.

For example, the point estimate definition formula for lnRoM——the log-transformed ratio of means——is quite simple, with \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) equivalent to the mean of an experimental and a control group respectively and \(yi\) indicating the resulting effect size point estimate:

\[yi = log(\frac{m_1}{m_2})\] The sampling variance for lnRoM is calculated with a second formula that was derived with what’s called the Delta method (spooky Calculus). Let \(sd_1\), \(sd_2\), and \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) present the standard deviation and sample size of the experimental and control groups respectively.

\[vi = \frac{sd_1^2}{n_1 m_1^2} + \frac{sd_2^2}{n_2 m_2^2}\] However, these estimators can produce biased estimates when sample size is low. For that reason, the default estimator used by most analysts is slightly more complex, with an additional term that corrects for low sample size bias, which for lnRoM, takes into account standard deviation (\(sd_1\) and \(sd_2\)) and sample size of each group (\(n_1\) and \(n_2\)).

\[yi_{BC} = log(\frac{m_1}{m_2}) + 0.5(\frac{sd_1^2}{n_1 m_1^2} - \frac{sd_2^2}{n_2 m_2^2})\] These derived formulas are known as bias-corrected estimators and they exist for most effect sizes. However, the bias-corrected estimators sometimes are more biased. escalc uses the best known estimator for each effect size type, which is usually the bias-corrected estimator (but not always and its not always clear which version of an effect size escalc uses).

To calculate lnRoM in R, we use escalc as follows:

escalc(measure = "ROM",
       m1i = 10, m2i = 6,
       sd1i = 1.5, sd2i = 1.5,
       n1i = 15, n2i = 15)

      yi     vi 
1 0.5108 0.0057

Our function, eff_this is similar but provides a little bit more control because it can return all formula versions, the exact formula used, and you can customize the effect size formula table to add custom formulas. Run the function to see the potential formulas support and their inputs:

Tip

Until recently, almost all effect size estimators used plugin formulas like shown above to calculate point estimates and sampling variance (which is what escalc does). However, growth in computational power has enabled alternative methods for calculating effect sizes and variance. In particular, the SAFE method has recently emerged as a powerful way to calculate bias-corrected point estimates and sampling variance for custom effect sizes and for effect sizes which lack a sampling variance formula.

SAFE is also essential for calculating point estimates and sampling variance for effect sizes for which differences of 0 (e.g., \(m1 - m2\)) lead to \(NA\) or \(Infinite\) values (Nakagawa et al 2026)—in particular the log magnitude (lnM) effect size, which describes the absolute difference between two groups (REF). For further information, including an interactive tutorial like this one, on the use of the SAFE method, please see Nakagawa et al. 2026 and the tutorial at github.com/ejlundgren/SAFE.

Other formulations

You’ll also notice, for example if looking at the documentation of escalc that there are many many effect size estimators based on different numeric properties of the data or the study design. Some of these are intercomparable. Others are not. This largely depends on whether the formulation affects the point estimate (effect size) or just the sampling variance estimate. We’ll cover many of these alternative versions in the effect size specific sections.

Paired design formulations

You’ll notice that there are alternative formulas for some effect sizes that can account for paired study designs. For example, before-after data or paired plot comparisons. These are common experimental designs in ecology and evolution but many meta-analyses do not take into account paired designs.

Some paired-design formulas produce effect sizes and sampling variance estimates that are comparable to those produced from unpaired formulas. In these cases, the effect size point estimate will be the same regardless of the paired/unpaired formulation. However, the sampling variance for the paired design will be lower—which will give paired design data more weight in downstream meta-analytic models.

However, there are also paired design formulations that are NOT comparable to unpaired formulas. These take into account the pairing in the point estimate formula and produce effect size estimates that need to be analyzed separately.

We try to clear up this confusion in the Appendix with some charts of compatibility between the different estimators in escalc ((sec?)).
